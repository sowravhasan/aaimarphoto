Myanmar: The Fight for Democracy

Yangon, Myanmar | February 2021 - Present

On February 1, 2021, Myanmar's military (Tatmadaw) staged a coup, arresting civilian leaders including State Counselor Aung San Suu Kyi and President Win Myint, effectively ending the country's decade-long transition to democracy. The coup came just hours before the newly elected parliament was set to convene, with the military claiming widespread electoral fraud in the November 2020 elections.

The coup sparked immediate and sustained resistance from the Myanmar people. What began as peaceful protests quickly evolved into a nationwide civil disobedience movement (CDM), with civil servants, healthcare workers, teachers, and citizens from all walks of life refusing to work under military rule. The iconic three-finger salute, borrowed from "The Hunger Games," became a symbol of resistance.

The military's response to protests has been brutal and systematic. Security forces have used live ammunition against demonstrators, conducted nighttime raids, and imposed martial law in several townships. According to human rights organizations, over 4,000 people have been killed and more than 20,000 arrested since the coup, including children, journalists, and medical workers.

The resistance movement has evolved from peaceful protests to armed resistance. The National Unity Government (NUG), formed by ousted parliamentarians, declared a "people's defensive war" and established the People's Defense Force (PDF). Local resistance groups have emerged across the country, engaging in guerrilla warfare against military installations and government infrastructure.

The coup has devastated Myanmar's economy and society. International sanctions, capital flight, and the civil disobedience movement have crippled economic activity. The banking system has largely collapsed, inflation has soared, and millions face food insecurity. The healthcare and education systems have been severely disrupted, with many professionals joining the CDM.

Ethnic minorities, already marginalized under previous governments, have faced increased persecution under military rule. The Rohingya in Rakhine State, Karen in the east, and other ethnic groups have experienced intensified military operations, forced displacement, and attacks on civilian areas. The junta has used airstrikes and artillery against ethnic areas with unprecedented frequency.

Despite international condemnation and sanctions, the military has shown no signs of backing down. ASEAN's limited engagement and China and Russia's continued support have emboldened the junta. As Myanmar enters its fourth year under military rule, the country remains trapped in a cycle of violence and resistance, with no clear resolution in sight and millions of people paying the price for the military's refusal to respect democratic will.
