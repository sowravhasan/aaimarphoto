Ethiopia: Tigray Conflict

Tigray Region, Ethiopia | November 2020 - November 2022

The conflict in Ethiopia's Tigray region, which lasted from November 2020 to November 2022, became one of the deadliest wars of the 21st century. The war pitted the Ethiopian federal government, supported by Eritrean forces and Amhara regional forces, against the Tigray People's Liberation Front (TPLF), resulting in hundreds of thousands of deaths and widespread atrocities.

The conflict's roots lie in decades of political tensions between the TPLF, which dominated Ethiopian politics for nearly three decades, and Prime Minister Abiy Ahmed's government, which came to power in 2018 promising democratic reforms. The TPLF's resistance to federal reforms and its decision to hold regional elections in defiance of federal postponement due to COVID-19 escalated tensions significantly.

The war officially began on November 4, 2020, when Ethiopian federal forces launched operations against the TPLF following alleged attacks on federal military bases. What was initially described as a law enforcement operation quickly evolved into a full-scale conflict involving multiple parties, including Eritrean Defense Forces and Amhara special forces fighting alongside federal troops.

The humanitarian crisis that unfolded was catastrophic. The region was subjected to a de facto siege, with communications cut, banking services suspended, and humanitarian access severely restricted. Millions of people were displaced, with hundreds of thousands fleeing to neighboring Sudan. Food became weaponized, with agricultural areas destroyed and aid deliveries blocked, leading to famine conditions in parts of Tigray.

Reports of widespread atrocities emerged from the conflict zone, including mass killings, systematic rape, and ethnic cleansing. The massacre in Axum, the destruction of cultural and religious sites, and the targeting of civilians based on their ethnicity highlighted the conflict's devastating impact on Tigray's population. International investigations later confirmed evidence of war crimes and crimes against humanity committed by all parties.

The involvement of Eritrean forces added a regional dimension to the conflict. Despite initial denials, it became clear that Eritrean troops were actively participating in the war, settling old scores with the TPLF and committing numerous atrocities against Tigrayan civilians. This foreign involvement complicated peace efforts and intensified the humanitarian crisis.

The conflict officially ended with the signing of the Cessation of Hostilities Agreement in Pretoria, South Africa, on November 2, 2022. While this agreement has largely held, the process of implementing its provisions, including the restoration of services, humanitarian access, and accountability for atrocities, remains ongoing. The war's legacy continues to affect Ethiopia's stability and the lives of millions of Tigrayans seeking to rebuild their shattered communities.
