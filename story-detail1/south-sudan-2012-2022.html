South Sudan | A Decade of Struggle

South Sudan's journey as the world's youngest nation has been marked by immense challenges since its independence in 2011. From 2012 to 2022, this landlocked country in East Africa has faced civil war, humanitarian crises, economic collapse, and the ongoing struggle to build functional state institutions.

The civil war that erupted in December 2013 between forces loyal to President Salva Kiir and those supporting former Vice President Riek Machar devastated the country. What began as a political dispute quickly devolved into ethnic violence, primarily between the Dinka and Nuer communities, though it affected all of South Sudan's diverse population.

The conflict has displaced millions of people, creating one of Africa's largest refugee crises. Entire villages have been abandoned as people flee to neighboring countries or internally displaced persons (IDP) camps. The Protection of Civilians (PoC) sites, established by the UN Mission in South Sudan (UNMISS), have become permanent homes for hundreds of thousands of people.

Oil production, the backbone of South Sudan's economy, has been severely disrupted by the conflict. Oil fields have been damaged or abandoned, and the crucial pipeline to Port Sudan has been repeatedly shut down. This has left the government with limited resources to provide basic services to its population, exacerbating the humanitarian crisis.

The humanitarian situation remains dire, with widespread food insecurity affecting millions. The conflict has disrupted agricultural activities, while flooding and droughts have further complicated efforts to achieve food security. Malnutrition rates, particularly among children, remain alarmingly high throughout the country.

Despite multiple peace agreements, including the 2018 Revitalized Agreement on the Resolution of the Conflict in South Sudan (R-ARCSS), implementation has been slow and incomplete. The formation of a unity government in 2020 was a positive step, but fundamental issues of governance, security sector reform, and constitutional making remain unresolved.

As South Sudan approaches its second decade of independence, the challenges remain immense. The country continues to grapple with building state capacity, achieving lasting peace, and addressing the humanitarian needs of its population while working toward economic recovery and development.
