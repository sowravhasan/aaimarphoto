Myanmar: Fight for Democracy

Yangon & Naypyidaw, Myanmar | February 2021 - Present

Myanmar's fragile transition to democracy came to an abrupt end on February 1, 2021, when the military staged a coup, detaining civilian leaders including State Counsellor Aung San Suu Kyi and President Win Myint. The military, known as the Tatmadaw, justified the takeover by claiming widespread fraud in the November 2020 elections, despite international observers finding no evidence of irregularities.

The coup sparked an unprecedented nationwide resistance movement, with millions of people taking to the streets in the largest protests since the 2007 Saffron Revolution. The Civil Disobedience Movement (CDM) saw doctors, teachers, civil servants, and workers from all sectors abandon their posts, effectively bringing the country to a standstill in defiance of military rule.

The military's response has been brutally violent, with security forces using live ammunition against peaceful protesters. Over 4,000 people have been killed and more than 20,000 arrested since the coup, including children, healthcare workers, and journalists. The military has imposed martial law in many areas and conducted nightly raids to arrest suspected dissidents.

Armed resistance has emerged across the country, with the formation of People's Defense Forces (PDFs) in many regions. These civilian militias, often with basic weapons, have engaged in guerrilla warfare against military forces. The shadow National Unity Government (NUG), formed by ousted lawmakers, has declared a "people's defensive war" against the junta.

The humanitarian crisis has deepened as the military has targeted civilians, including attacking schools, hospitals, and religious buildings. Over 1.5 million people have been internally displaced, while the economy has collapsed, pushing millions into poverty. Basic services like healthcare and education have been severely disrupted.

International sanctions have isolated Myanmar's military leadership, but the junta has shown no signs of backing down. China and Russia have provided diplomatic cover, while ASEAN has struggled to find an effective response beyond diplomatic pressure. The military's control remains contested in large parts of the country.

Myanmar's fight for democracy has become a defining struggle of the 21st century, pitting the determination of ordinary citizens against military might. Despite facing overwhelming odds, the people of Myanmar continue to resist, believing that their sacrifices will eventually restore the democratic future that was stolen from them.
