South Sudan: 2012-2022

Juba & Unity State, South Sudan | 2012 - 2022

South Sudan's independence in 2011 was supposed to mark the end of decades of conflict and the beginning of a new chapter for Africa's newest nation. However, the period from 2012 to 2022 was defined by a devastating civil war, economic collapse, and humanitarian crisis that left the country struggling to survive as a functioning state.

The conflict began in December 2013 when political tensions between President Salva Kiir and Vice President Riek Machar escalated into violence. What started as a political dispute quickly took on ethnic dimensions, with fighting largely along Dinka and Nuer ethnic lines, drawing in various armed groups and spreading across the country.

The war was characterized by extreme brutality, with widespread reports of mass killings, sexual violence, and the recruitment of child soldiers. Villages were burned, civilians were targeted based on their ethnicity, and both sides committed atrocities that shocked the international community and led to war crimes investigations.

The humanitarian impact was catastrophic, with over 400,000 people killed and more than 4 million displaced from their homes. South Sudan became one of the world's largest refugee crises, with millions seeking safety in neighboring Uganda, Kenya, Ethiopia, and Sudan. Those who remained faced severe food insecurity and limited access to basic services.

Despite multiple peace agreements and international mediation efforts, including a unity government formed in 2020, the fundamental issues that sparked the conflict remained largely unresolved. Oil revenues, which account for nearly all government income, were decimated by the conflict and falling global prices.

The international community, including the UN peacekeeping mission UNMISS, struggled to protect civilians and facilitate humanitarian access. Regional organizations like IGAD played key roles in peace negotiations, but implementation of agreements proved challenging due to ongoing mistrust and competition for resources.

As South Sudan marked its first decade of independence in 2021, the nation faced enormous challenges in building sustainable peace, establishing effective governance, and addressing the deep wounds left by years of conflict. The resilience of the South Sudanese people, however, offered hope for a more stable and prosperous future.
