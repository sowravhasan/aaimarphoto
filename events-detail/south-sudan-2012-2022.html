South Sudan: 2012-2022

Juba, South Sudan | 2012 - 2022

South Sudan, the world's youngest nation, has endured a decade of devastating civil war since its independence in 2011. What began as a political dispute between President Salva Kiir and his former deputy Riek Machar in December 2013 quickly spiraled into a brutal conflict that has torn apart the fabric of this nascent country.

The civil war has been characterized by extreme violence, including ethnic killings, sexual violence, and the recruitment of child soldiers. Hundreds of thousands of people have been killed, while millions more have been displaced from their homes, creating one of Africa's largest refugee crises and putting immense strain on neighboring countries.

The humanitarian situation in South Sudan remains dire, with more than half the population facing severe food insecurity. Climate change, including devastating floods and droughts, has compounded the crisis, destroying crops and livestock and making it even more difficult for communities to recover and rebuild.

Despite multiple peace agreements and international mediation efforts, sustainable peace has remained elusive. The most recent peace deal, signed in 2018, has seen some progress but continues to face significant challenges in implementation, with ongoing violence in several regions and slow progress on key reforms.

The international community has provided substantial humanitarian assistance, but access to affected populations remains limited due to ongoing insecurity and poor infrastructure. International peacekeepers have worked to protect civilians, but their mandate and resources have been tested by the scale and complexity of the crisis.

The story of South Sudan over the past decade is one of immense suffering but also remarkable resilience. Despite facing unimaginable hardships, communities continue to hope for peace and work toward rebuilding their lives, while the international community grapples with how to support lasting stability in this troubled nation.
