Ethiopia: The Tigray Conflict

Tigray Region, Ethiopia | November 2020 - November 2022

The Tigray conflict began in November 2020 when Ethiopian Prime Minister Abiy Ahmed ordered military operations against the Tigray People's Liberation Front (TPLF), the ruling party of the northern Tigray region. What started as a political dispute escalated into a devastating war that would claim hundreds of thousands of lives and create one of the world's worst humanitarian crises.

The conflict's roots lay in tensions between the federal government and the TPLF, which had dominated Ethiopian politics for nearly three decades before Abiy came to power in 2018. The TPLF's decision to hold regional elections in defiance of federal postponement due to COVID-19 provided the immediate trigger for military intervention.

As fighting intensified, forces from neighboring Eritrea entered the conflict on the side of the Ethiopian government, while armed groups from the Amhara region also participated. The involvement of multiple actors transformed what might have been a localized conflict into a complex regional war with devastating consequences for civilians.

Reports of mass killings, sexual violence, and ethnic cleansing emerged from the region, though a communications blackout and restricted access for journalists and aid workers made verification difficult. The Ethiopian government and TPLF traded accusations of atrocities, while international investigators later confirmed widespread violations of international humanitarian law by all parties.

The humanitarian impact was catastrophic, with millions of people displaced from their homes and in need of emergency assistance. Access to basic services collapsed, hospitals were destroyed or looted, and a de facto blockade prevented aid from reaching those in desperate need, creating conditions that experts warned could lead to famine.

International pressure for peace intensified as evidence of the conflict's severity mounted. The African Union mediated talks between the warring parties, leading to a cessation of hostilities agreement signed in Pretoria, South Africa, in November 2022. The agreement marked the beginning of efforts to restore peace and begin the long process of reconstruction and reconciliation.

The Tigray conflict highlighted the fragility of Ethiopia's federal system and the challenges facing Africa's second-most populous nation. As the country begins to heal from this devastating war, the task of addressing its root causes and preventing future conflicts remains a critical challenge for Ethiopian society and the international community.
