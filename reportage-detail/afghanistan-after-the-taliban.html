Afghanistan: After the Taliban

Kabul, Afghanistan | August 2021 - Present

The fall of Kabul on August 15, 2021, marked the end of a 20-year Western military presence in Afghanistan and the return of Taliban rule. The chaotic evacuation from Hamid Karzai International Airport became a symbol of the abrupt end to America's longest war and the beginning of a new, uncertain chapter for the Afghan people.

The Taliban's swift takeover surprised many observers, as provincial capitals fell in rapid succession leading up to the capture of Kabul. The Afghan National Defense and Security Forces, despite years of training and billions in investment, collapsed almost entirely as government officials fled the country and soldiers abandoned their posts.

For Afghan women and girls, the Taliban's return has meant a systematic rollback of rights gained over two decades. Universities have been closed to women, most female workers have been barred from their jobs, and women have been prohibited from most forms of employment except in essential services like healthcare.

The humanitarian crisis that followed has been severe. The freezing of Afghan assets abroad and the suspension of international aid led to economic collapse, widespread unemployment, and food insecurity affecting millions of Afghans. International efforts to provide humanitarian assistance while avoiding legitimizing the Taliban government have created a delicate balance.

Despite promises of a more moderate approach, the Taliban's governance has largely mirrored their previous rule in the 1990s, with strict interpretation of Islamic law, public executions, and severe restrictions on personal freedoms. The international community continues to grapple with how to engage with a regime that controls Afghanistan but lacks widespread international recognition.

The story of Afghanistan after the Taliban's return is one of a nation struggling to find stability while its people face uncertainty about their future. For many Afghans, particularly women and minorities, the promises of progress and democracy that defined the previous two decades have given way to a reality that echoes the country's tumultuous past.
